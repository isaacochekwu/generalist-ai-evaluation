# Hallucination Detection in General-Purpose AI Outputs

A guide to identifying, classifying, and documenting hallucinations in non-medical AI outputs across writing, reasoning, Q&A, and summarisation tasks.

---

## What is a Hallucination?

A hallucination is content generated by an AI model that is factually incorrect, fabricated, or ungrounded — but presented with the same confidence as accurate information. The model does not "know" it is wrong; it generates plausible-sounding content based on statistical patterns, not verified facts.

Hallucinations are distinct from:
- **Opinions** — subjective claims that are not verifiable as true/false
- **Uncertainty** — hedged claims where the model acknowledges it may be wrong
- **Errors of reasoning** — logical mistakes based on correct premises

---

## Hallucination Types in General AI Outputs

### Type 1: Phantom Citation
The model invents a source, paper, book, article, or study that does not exist.

**Example:**
> "According to a 2021 study published in the Journal of Cognitive Science by Dr. Alan Mercer, multitasking reduces productivity by 47%."

The study, journal, and author may all be fabricated, or exist but not contain this claim.

**Detection:** Search for the cited source. If it cannot be verified in under 60 seconds via a basic search, flag it.

---

### Type 2: Fabricated Quote
The model attributes a quote to a real person that they never said.

**Example:**
> "As Einstein famously said, 'The measure of intelligence is the ability to change.'"

This quote is widely misattributed and not verified as Einstein's. Models frequently generate plausible-sounding quotes from famous figures.

**Detection:** Famous quotes from real people should always be verified. If a quote feels too perfectly aligned with the essay's argument, it may be fabricated.

---

### Type 3: False Statistic
The model generates a specific numeric claim (percentage, count, date, ranking) with no factual basis.

**Example:**
> "Over 78% of remote workers report higher productivity than in-office counterparts."

A specific statistic with no source is a common hallucination pattern. The number is generated to sound authoritative, not retrieved from data.

**Detection:** Any specific percentage, count, or ranking presented without a verifiable source should be flagged.

---

### Type 4: Entity Confusion
The model conflates two real entities — people, organisations, places, events — producing hybrid misinformation.

**Example:**
> "Elon Musk founded Amazon in 1994 to revolutionise e-commerce."

This merges accurate facts about Amazon (founded 1994, e-commerce) with an incorrect founder (Bezos, not Musk).

**Detection:** For any factual claim about a named entity, mentally verify the key attributes (founder, date, location, role) independently.

---

### Type 5: Temporal Hallucination
The model presents outdated information as current, or incorrectly places events in time.

**Example:**
> "The current Prime Minister of the UK is Boris Johnson."

This was accurate as of 2021–2022 but is factually wrong after that. Models with training cutoffs frequently hallucinate current-state information.

**Detection:** For any "current" status claim (who holds a role, what a policy is, what is the latest version), flag if the model does not acknowledge its knowledge cutoff.

---

### Type 6: Plausible but Unverifiable Detail
The model adds specific details that make an output feel well-researched but cannot be verified.

**Example:**
> "The company was founded in a garage in Austin, Texas, in March 2003 by two former Google engineers."

Every detail sounds credible, but each may be fabricated. This type is the hardest to catch because nothing in the output triggers an obvious alarm.

**Detection:** When evaluating factual outputs, treat all specific details (dates, locations, names, roles) as suspects until verified. The more specific a claim, the higher the hallucination risk.

---

## Hallucination Risk by Task Type

| Task Type | Hallucination Risk | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| Creative writing | Low | No factual claims required |
| Opinion / persuasive essay | Low–Medium | Facts used as support are at risk |
| Summarisation | Medium | Model may add details not in source |
| Q&A (factual) | High | Direct factual claims; high hallucination exposure |
| Research / citation | Very High | Sources, quotes, statistics all at risk |
| "Current state" queries | Very High | Model cannot know post-training facts |

---

## Hallucination vs. Outdated Information

A model stating something that was true at training but is no longer true is **not a hallucination** — it is an outdated fact. This still requires a flag (especially for "current" queries), but the model should not be penalised as heavily as for fabricated content.

**Hallucination:** The model invents something that was never true.  
**Outdated:** The model states something that was true but is no longer current.

Both require correction. Only hallucination requires rejection-level severity.

## Documentation Format for Hallucinations

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Type | (1–6 from taxonomy above) |
| Claim | Direct quote of the hallucinated content |
| Why it's wrong | Brief explanation of the factual error |
| Verifiable? | Can the correct fact be confirmed? (Yes / No / Unclear) |
| Correct information | The accurate fact, if known |
| Severity | Low / Medium / High / Critical |
| Recommended action | Flag for feedback / Reject / Escalate |
